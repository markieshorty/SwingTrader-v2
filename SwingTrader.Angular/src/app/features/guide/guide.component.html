How Cadentic works

This guide explains what the system does and shows your account's current settings inline, so it always reflects how your bot is actually configured. You're currently in {{ currentMode() }} mode.

The daily pipeline

Cadentic runs as a chain of scheduled agents. Each does one job and hands off to the next. All times listed below are in ET, the market's timezone (times elsewhere in the app are shown in your local time).

  1. Watchlist (weekly, Sun 20:00) — screens the S&P 500 / Nasdaq 100 down to a focused list of candidates.
  2. Research (daily, 06:30) — scores every watchlist symbol 0–10 on the six gate components in the next tab, using fresh pre-market data. An optional midday rescore (off by default) refreshes the same day's scores at 12:30.
  3. Report (daily, 08:30) — emails you a brief of the day's buy/watch/hold/avoid signals.
  4. Approval — if enabled, nothing trades until you approve. Approvals are on the Trades page. One approval covers the whole trading day, including any midday-rescored signals — you are never re-asked intraday.
  5. Execution (daily, from 09:20) — places orders for approved buy signals at Trading 212.
  6. Monitor (every 5 min, 09:30–16:00) — watches open positions and enforces stops, targets, trailing stops and exits.
  7. Refinement (monthly) — correlates each component's scores against your trade outcomes and proposes weight adjustments.

How a signal is scored

Every candidate stock gets a single conviction score from 0 to 10 — the higher the number, the stronger the case for buying it. That score is a weighted blend of six separate chart checks (the gate). Each check scores the stock from 0 to 1, that score is multiplied by the check's weight (how much it counts), and the results are added up and scaled to 10.

The weights below are your account's current values and add up to 100%. They aren't fixed — the Refinement agent gradually shifts weight toward whichever checks have actually predicted your winning trades. Tap any check to see what it means in plain terms. Three further checks — news sentiment, fundamental momentum and filing language — sit outside this gate score; they size and veto the trade instead, and are explained in the Risk & your money tab.

What the score decides

  • Score ≥ {{ weights()?.buyThreshold ?? '—' }} → Buy — strong enough to actually trade (subject to approval).
  • Score ≥ {{ weights()?.watchThreshold ?? '—' }} → Watch — interesting, kept on the radar but not traded.
  • Below that → Hold / Avoid — not acted on.

If a check can't get its data (say a news feed is down), it counts as a neutral 0.5 rather than dragging the score down — so a missing data point can never nudge a stock toward a Buy it didn't earn.

Regime books — one risk profile per market backdrop

Risk settings aren't a single fixed profile any more. The system keeps a separate book of risk settings for each market regime — Bull, Neutral, Bear and Crisis — so it can carry a full position in a healthy market and automatically tighten up (smaller positions, fewer of them, more locked capital, or pause entirely) when the backdrop turns. Every day the system reads the S&P 500 against its moving averages and volatility, decides which regime you're in, and the matching book takes over. You edit these books on Settings → Risk management, one tab per regime.

The Default master book

If you'd rather run one risk profile regardless of the regime, switch on the Default book. When it's enabled it overrides all four regime books — the system stops switching books by regime and uses Default for every decision. It's the simplest way to run the account: set your risk once and it always applies. The Default book is currently {{ defaultBookOn() ? Autopause — sitting a regime out

Each book carries an autopause switch. When the governing book has autopause on, the system keeps monitoring and managing open positions but opens no new ones until a book without autopause takes over (typically as the market improves). It's how you tell the bot "don't start anything new while conditions look like this" — usually set on the Bear and Crisis books, off on Bull and Neutral. Distress quarantine — the doomed-stock guard

Separately from any score, a small set of hard red flags quarantines a company outright: an SEC 8-K announcing a delisting notice, bankruptcy or receivership, or that prior financial statements can no longer be relied on — plus going-concern language appearing in its annual/quarterly report. These are detected from the filings themselves (no AI judgement involved). While a flag is active (~90 days from the filing), a Buy signal is demoted to Watch and any open position in the name is sold — a company fighting for survival is not a swing-trade candidate, however good the chart looks.

How positions are exited

Buying is only half the job — knowing when to sell is what protects the account. Once a trade is open the Monitor agent watches it continuously and closes it automatically when any of the following triggers. You never have to sell manually. Each position locks its rules in at buy time — hold limits, probation and trailing-stop shape, like the stop and target — so changing a setting affects positions opened from then on, never ones already open.

The stop, profit target and guide hold are set per setup type — an oversold recovery and a breakout can carry different stops and holds because they behave differently. You tune these on Settings → Setup tactics, and the Strategy Lab can search for better values and apply them for you. Stop loss — caps the downside {{ pct(weights()?.stopLossPctDefault) }} below entry

A price at which the trade is sold automatically to stop a small loss becoming a big one. If you buy at £100 and the stop is {{ pct(weights()?.stopLossPctDefault) }}, the position sells if the price falls to roughly £{{ stopExample() }}. Every trade has one — it's the single most important risk control.

Profit target — takes the win ≈ 8–12% above entry

The price at which the trade is sold to bank a profit. It's set per-trade from the setup type and how strong the conviction was (stronger signals aim a little higher). If price hits the target, Monitor sells and locks in the gain.

Trailing stop — protects a winner arms at +{{ pct(risk()?.trailingActivationPct) }}, trails by {{ pct(risk()?.trailingDistancePct) }}

Once a trade is up by {{ pct(risk()?.trailingActivationPct) }}, a second stop switches on that follows the price upward but never moves back down. It sits {{ pct(risk()?.trailingDistancePct) }} below the highest price reached. So if a winner keeps climbing the stop climbs with it, and if the price then drops {{ pct(risk()?.trailingDistancePct) }} from its peak the trade sells — letting winners run while still banking most of the gain if they reverse.

Guide hold — a soft checkpoint, not a hard deadline ~{{ risk()?.maxHoldDays ?? '—' }} days

Each setup has a guide hold — the number of days a trade is expected to take to play out (set per setup on the Settings page; the figure shown here is your typical regime value). Reaching it does not force a sale: a position still showing healthy momentum keeps running as a runner. What it does is hand the trade over to the daily momentum check below, which decides whether to let it run or close it. As a final backstop, a runner is force-closed once it reaches {{ hardHoldCeiling(risk()?.maxHoldDays) ?? '—' }} days (2.5× the guide hold) so capital can never be tied up indefinitely.

Momentum health check — carries runners, exits a broken thesis from day {{ risk()?.minHoldDays ?? '—' }}

After a minimum hold of {{ risk()?.minHoldDays ?? '—' }} days, Monitor re-checks whether the reasons it bought still hold. If the momentum that justified the trade has clearly broken down, it exits early rather than waiting for the full stop loss to trigger. This same check is what decides a position's fate once it passes its guide hold: still healthy → it keeps running as a runner; stalled → it's closed to free up the capital.

Earnings blackout — avoids a coin-flip {{ risk()?.earningsGateDays ?? '—' }} days

The bot won't open a new position within {{ risk()?.earningsGateDays ?? '—' }} days of a company's earnings announcement. Earnings are unpredictable, all-or-nothing events that can gap the price far past any stop loss overnight — not the kind of risk this strategy takes.

Two completely separate worlds

You're currently in {{ currentMode() }} mode. Demo trades practice money against Trading 212's demo environment; Live trades real money. They use different API credentials and are kept entirely separate — trades, reports, refinement and P&L for one mode never mix into the other.

  • Each mode needs its own Trading 212 key + secret pair (demo keys are rejected by the live endpoint and vice-versa).
  • Switching mode takes effect immediately and reloads the app so every figure reflects the new mode.
  • If you switch with positions still open in the current mode, you'll be warned first — the bot stops monitoring those positions until you switch back, so they'd sit without stop-loss protection.

Tip: use the Test button on Settings → API Keys. It calls Trading 212 and shows the account balance and whether it's practice or real money — the quickest way to confirm your keys are correct and not swapped.

What the Strategy Lab is for

The Strategy Lab lets you test the strategy against history before risking anything live. You pick a symbol set and a date range, run a simulation, and see how the rules would have performed — win rate, expectancy, drawdown, equity curve and a trade-by-trade log. When a run looks better than what you're running now, one button applies its settings to your live configuration.

Everything here is a simulation on past data. Past performance never guarantees future results, and a strategy tuned too tightly to history often disappoints live — which is exactly what the validation modes below guard against.

The run modes

  • Replay / Historic — run the current strategy over a past period and read the results.
  • A/B compare — run two configurations side by side (e.g. current vs a tweak, or with a setup type filtered out) and see which wins on the same data.
  • Optimizer sweep — automatically searches combinations of weights, thresholds, position size, locked capital and per-setup tactics, and ranks them. It only proposes settings that keep the book valid to apply.
  • Validate (train / holdout) — tunes on one slice of history and checks the result on a separate, unseen slice, so you know a good number isn't just curve-fitting.
  • Monte Carlo — reshuffles the trade sequence many times to show the range of outcomes, not just the one lucky (or unlucky) path history happened to take.
  • Setup contribution — removes one setup type at a time to reveal which patterns actually help and which are a drag.
  • Regime comparison — runs each regime book (and Default) over the same period so you can see how each risk profile would have held up.

Regimes in a backtest

Because risk is now organised into per-regime books, a run lets you choose which book drives the simulation from a dropdown: a single fixed book, the Default master book, or Mixed — where the simulation classifies each historical day's regime and switches to the matching book as it goes, the closest match to how live trading actually behaves. Autopause and locked-capital rules are honoured in the simulation just as they are live.

Applying a result

When a run produces settings you want to keep, the Apply button writes them into your live configuration — weights and thresholds, per-setup tactics, and the risk levers. Applied risk settings land on the book the run was tuned against: the Default book if it's enabled, otherwise the Neutral book. Nothing is applied silently — you choose to apply, and you can always re-tune.

Recommended workflow: validate or Monte-Carlo a promising configuration before applying it, then run it as a shadow/forward check for a while rather than flipping straight to live — a result that only looks good on one historical path is the main thing to avoid.
